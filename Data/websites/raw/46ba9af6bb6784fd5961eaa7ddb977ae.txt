<p>Mr Qi said party members were generally "outstanding employees" in senior roles.</p><p>At a rare media briefing on Thursday, he said: "Setting up party organisations within enterprises complies with China's laws and regulations," and was a "natural practice".</p><p>The party leadership had asked party units to "integrate their practice with company practices" and "provide strong support".</p><p>In his report to the 19th congress of the Chinese Communist Party, Mr Xi said the party had a central role in guiding China to becoming a global power by 2050.</p><p>The crackdown against corrupt party members would continue, with a watchdog applying rules that are "stricter and tougher than the law", said Yang Xiaodu, deputy secretary for the Central Commission for Discipline Inspection.</p><p>The Communist Party has a special system of discipline for party members that sits outside the legal system.</p><p>The discipline commission had punished 1.5 million party members after complaints about 2.6 million members. Six congress delegates were blocked from attending the twice-a-decade meeting after last minute telephone and letter complaints were received about their behaviour.</p><p>But China will end the controversial practice of "shuanggui" - a tool of party discipline, under which corruption suspects can disappear for months as they are interrogated in secret locations. It had been labelled "abusive" by human rights groups.</p><p>Mr Xi's report to the congress contained the statement that shuanggui will be replaced by legal detention.</p><p>Sophie Richardson, China director at Human Rights Watch, said: "If President Xi's proposal means that detainees are not ill-treated, get to choose their lawyers, and otherwise have their rights respected, then this will indeed be a significant step forward."</p><p>"But if he is just proposing to replace one abusive detention system with another, it will be another setback for legal reform in China."</p><p>Shuanggui has been a factor in the reluctance of international police forces to cooperate with China in the extradition of corruption suspects who flee overseas. It was also a factor behind the blocking of an extradition treaty between Australia and China in parliament.</p><p>A new national law establishing a National Supervision Commission to oversee corruption investigations as a state agency, and not a party discipline body, is expected.</p><p>This distinction is seen as important by international police forces who can't deal with a political discipline body.</p><p>Mr Xi's report said the supervisory commission will "share offices and work together with the Party's disciplinary inspection commission", and have investigation powers under the law.</p><p>But it will also enable corruption investigations to be expanded beyond party members, to cover "everyone in the public sector who exercises public power".</p><p>Mr Yang said the new supervisory commission would work "under the leadership of the party".</p></div>
