        <div class="body-copy">
            <p>President Xi Jinping walked onto the red carpet of China&#x2019;s Great Hall of the People on Wednesday having amassed more power than any leader in a generation.</p><div class="regwall" data-position="1"></div><figure data-type="image" data-id="319559319" data-image-type="photo" data-image-size="full" data-align="center" style="max-width: 1803px;" class="figure-expandable"><div class="image"><div id="lazy-img-319559319" class="lazy-img"><style>#lazy-img-319559319:before{padding-top:66.99944536882973%;}</style><img src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/ilJk3lbGuvgk/v5/60x-1.jpg" data-native-src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/ilJk3lbGuvgk/v5/-1x-1.jpg" class="lazy-img__image"></div></div><figcaption><div class="news-figure-caption-text caption"><p>Xi Jinping, Han Zheng, Wang Huning, Li Zhanshu, Li Keqiang, Wang Yang and Zhao Leji.</p></div><div class="news-figure-credit credit">Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg</div></figcaption></figure><p>Behind him followed -- in order of rank -- the other six members of the Politburo Standing Committee, the panel that meets weekly to manage the affairs of almost one-fifth of the world&#x2019;s population. The new line-up chosen after the Communist Party&#x2019;s twice-a-decade congress surrounds Xi with loyalists to advance his ambitious plans to cement one-party rule and complete China&#x2019;s reemergence as a great power.</p><div class="regwall" data-position="2"></div><p>Here&#x2019;s a look at the officials who will run China for the next five years, in the order they appeared:</p><div class="regwall" data-position="3"></div><h3>Xi Jinping, 64</h3><figure data-type="image" data-id="319565619" data-image-type="photo" data-image-size="full" data-align="center" style="max-width: 2000px;" class="figure-expandable"><div class="image"><div id="lazy-img-319565619" class="lazy-img"><style>#lazy-img-319565619:before{padding-top:66.7%;}</style><img src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/iYx2DjxhvIYE/v0/60x-1.jpg" data-native-src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/iYx2DjxhvIYE/v0/-1x-1.jpg" class="lazy-img__image"></div></div><figcaption><div class="news-figure-caption-text caption"><p></p></div><div class="news-figure-credit credit">Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg</div></figcaption></figure>
        <div id="box-QrDWJaz" class="page-ad" data-position="mobile-box">
            <script class="params" type="application/json">{"contentId":"OWGENF6JIJUP01","position":"box","dimensions":{"mobile":[[300,250],[1,1]]},"type":"Mobile Body Box Ad","positionIncrement":1,"targeting":{"position":"box1","positionIncrement":1,"url":"/news/articles/2017-10-25/the-seven-men-who-will-rule-china-for-the-next-five-years"},"containerId":"box-QrDWJaz"}</script>

        <p>For Xi, the party congress was a crowning moment. The enshrinement of his name in the party&#x2019;s charter capped a decades-long journey from being forced to live in the countryside under Mao Zedong to becoming a leader on par with him. In his first five years in power, Xi has demonstrated vast ambitions to restore China&#x2019;s place among the great powers, laying out a three-decade plan to finish the job. His changes to the party&#x2019;s governing documents -- and the lack of a clear heir -- position him to rule China for years to come.</p><div class="regwall" data-position="4"></div><h3>Li Keqiang, 62</h3><figure data-type="image" data-id="319555093" data-image-type="photo" data-image-size="full" data-align="center" style="max-width: 2000px;" class="figure-expandable"><div class="image"><div id="lazy-img-319555093" class="lazy-img"><style>#lazy-img-319555093:before{padding-top:66.7%;}</style><img src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/iblbQdfXWUIg/v3/60x-1.jpg" data-native-src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/iblbQdfXWUIg/v3/-1x-1.jpg" class="lazy-img__image"></div></div><figcaption><div class="news-figure-caption-text caption"><p></p></div><div class="news-figure-credit credit">Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg</div></figcaption></figure><p>Once seen as a <a itemscope="itemscope" itemprop="StoryLink" href="/news/terminal/JQAT2G1A1I4I" title="China Names 4 New Top Leaders, Suggesting Hu&#x2019;s Heirs (Update2)" class="terminal-news-story" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><meta itemprop="active" content="true"><meta itemprop="type" content="StoryLink"><meta itemprop="suid" content="JQAT2G1A1I4I">contender</a> for the presidency, <a itemscope="itemscope" itemprop="StoryLink" href="https://www.bloomberg.com/politics/articles/2017-03-29/china-s-li-seen-staying-put-as-premier-instead-of-changing-roles" title="China&#x2019;s Li Seen as Unlikely to Lose Premier&#x2019;s Job After All (1)" target="_blank"><meta itemprop="active" content="true"><meta itemprop="type" content="StoryLink"><meta itemprop="suid" content="ONLT2H6K50Y3">Li Keqiang</a> watched Xi win the top job and instead became premier. The job appeared a natural fit for Li, who holds a Ph.D in economics and served as top lieutenant to former Premier Wen Jiabao. He once likened unleashing market forces to &#x201C;cutting one&#x2019;s wrist.&#x201D; His image <a itemscope="itemscope" itemprop="StoryLink" href="https://www.bloomberg.com/politics/articles/2017-03-29/china-s-li-seen-staying-put-as-premier-instead-of-changing-roles" title="China&#x2019;s Li Seen as Unlikely to Lose Premier&#x2019;s Job After All (1)" target="_blank"><meta itemprop="active" content="true"><meta itemprop="type" content="StoryLink"><meta itemprop="suid" content="ONLT2H6K50Y3">took a hit</a> during the 2015 stock market rout and Xi has quickly assumed economic roles held by past premiers. Still, if there&#x2019;s any gap between Xi and Li, outsiders haven&#x2019;t been allowed to see it.</p><h3>Li Zhanshu, 67</h3><figure data-type="image" data-id="319516749" data-image-type="photo" data-image-size="full" data-align="center" style="max-width: 2000px;" class="figure-expandable"><div class="image"><div id="lazy-img-319516749" class="lazy-img"><style>#lazy-img-319516749:before{padding-top:66.85%;}</style><img src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/ivLJnyIof4PM/v4/60x-1.jpg" data-native-src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/ivLJnyIof4PM/v4/-1x-1.jpg" class="lazy-img__image"></div></div><figcaption><div class="news-figure-caption-text caption"><p></p></div><div class="news-figure-credit credit">Source: Imaginechina</div></figcaption></figure><p>Not only&#xA0;is Li Zhanshu among an exclusive group of top officials who accompany Xi on diplomatic visits, he&#x2019;s also become the leader&#x2019;s personal liaison to Russian President Vladimir Putin. Before becoming Xi&#x2019;s chief of staff in 2012, Li toiled for decades in rural obscurity. He wrote poetry and held jobs in places like China&#x2019;s ancient heartland of Shaanxi and the rust belt province of Heilongjiang. His ties with Xi stretch back to the 1980s, when they served in adjacent counties in the central province of Hebei.</p><h3>Wang Yang, 62</h3><figure data-type="image" data-id="319514701" data-image-type="photo" data-image-size="full" data-align="center" style="max-width: 2000px;" class="figure-expandable"><div class="image"><div id="lazy-img-319514701" class="lazy-img"><style>#lazy-img-319514701:before{padding-top:67.4%;}</style><img src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/iZ_w_wbETC8c/v3/60x-1.jpg" data-native-src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/iZ_w_wbETC8c/v3/-1x-1.jpg" class="lazy-img__image"></div></div><figcaption><div class="news-figure-caption-text caption"><p></p></div><div class="news-figure-credit credit">Photographer: Andrew Harrer/Bloomberg</div></figcaption></figure><p>Wang Yang missed the cut for the Standing Committee in 2012, the year his effort to bring the pro-democracy protests in the fishing village of Wukan to a peaceful resolution helped earn him a spot on Time Magazine&#x2019;s <a itemscope="itemscope" itemprop="StoryLink" href="http://content.time.com/time/specials/packages/article/0,28804,2111975_2111976_2112002,00.htmlhttp://content.time.com/time/specials/packages/article/0,28804,2111975_2111976_2112002,00.html" title="Link to Entry" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><meta itemprop="type" content="WebLink">most-influential list</a>. The former dean of Fudan University Law School has advised three presidents and helped draft Jiang Zemin&#x2019;s &#x201C;three represents,&#x201D; which <a itemscope="itemscope" itemprop="StoryLink" href="/news/terminal/HHC85C07SXKX" title="China&#x2019;s Hu Drops Reform From Communist Party Birthday Speech" class="terminal-news-story" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><meta itemprop="active" content="true"><meta itemprop="type" content="StoryLink"><meta itemprop="suid" content="HHC85C07SXKX">drew</a>&#xA0;entrepreneurs, capitalists and intellectuals under the party&#x2019;s umbrella. As an academic, he studied power transitions and legal systems and advocated the strengths of strong central leadership over fractious democratic systems. His ascent to the Standing Committee dovetails with Xi&#x2019;s increased emphasis on communist ideology.</p><h3>Zhao Leji, 60</h3><figure data-type="image" data-id="319516485" data-image-type="photo" data-image-size="full" data-align="center" style="max-width: 3000px;" class="figure-expandable"><div class="image"><div id="lazy-img-319516485" class="lazy-img"><style>#lazy-img-319516485:before{padding-top:72.8%;}</style><img src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/iP6Jr4n8Vcqo/v3/60x-1.jpg" data-native-src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/iP6Jr4n8Vcqo/v3/-1x-1.jpg" class="lazy-img__image"></div></div><figcaption><div class="news-figure-caption-text caption"><p></p></div><div class="news-figure-credit credit">Photograpaher: Wang Zhao/AFP via Getty Images</div></figcaption></figure>
