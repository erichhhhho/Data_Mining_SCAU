            <figure align="center" data-align="center" data-id="319138946" data-image-size="column" data-type="image" style="max-width: 2200px;" class="figure-expandable"><div class="image"><div id="lazy-img-319138946" class="lazy-img"><style>#lazy-img-319138946:before{padding-top:66.68181818181817%;}</style><img src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/iWas2XDNf6ao/v1/60x-1.jpg" data-native-src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/iWas2XDNf6ao/v1/-1x-1.jpg" class="lazy-img__image"></div></div><figcaption><div class="news-figure-caption-text caption">Madame He Qiaonv.</div><div class="news-figure-credit credit">Source: Panthera</div></figcaption></figure><p>This Saturday, Oct. 14, in Monaco, He Qiaonv will announce the first step in a $1.5 billion plan that may represent the largest-ever personal philanthropic commitment to wildlife conservation.&#xA0;</p><div class="regwall" data-position="1"></div><p>The number isn&#x2019;t the only thing that&#x2019;s surprising about the announcement. The source might equally raise eyebrows: The donation isn&#x2019;t coming from a known&#xA0;Western conservationist like&#xA0;<a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2017-05-09/paul-allen-is-using-technology-to-save-the-african-elephant" itemprop="StoryLink" itemscope="itemscope" target="_blank"><meta content="StoryLink" itemprop="type">Paul Allen</a>, but from a landscape planner-turned-environmental steward who&#x2019;s based in Beijing.</p><div class="regwall" data-position="2"></div><figure align="left" data-align="left" data-id="319128930" data-image-size="column" data-type="image" class="figure-expandable"><div class="image"><div id="lazy-img-319128930" class="lazy-img"><style>#lazy-img-319128930:before{padding-top:150.27322404371586%;}</style><img src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/ivGlHhUlT9vY/v2/60x-1.jpg" data-native-src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/ivGlHhUlT9vY/v2/-1x-1.jpg" class="lazy-img__image"></div></div><figcaption><div class="news-figure-caption-text caption">Tom Kaplan,&#xA0;founder and chairman of Panthera.</div><div class="news-figure-credit credit">Photographer: Steve Winter/Panthera</div></figcaption></figure><p>Madame He represents a new wave of self-made Chinese philanthropists unafraid to spend; her seven-year pledge stands at more than a third of her current $3.6 billion net worth, according to the Bloomberg Billionaires Index.</p><div class="regwall" data-position="3"></div>
        <div id="box-RXz25Mf" class="page-ad" data-position="mobile-box">
            <script class="params" type="application/json">{"contentId":"OXQICA6S972A01","position":"box","dimensions":{"mobile":[[300,250],[1,1]]},"type":"Mobile Body Box Ad","positionIncrement":1,"targeting":{"position":"box1","positionIncrement":1,"url":"/news/articles/2017-10-12/beijing-philanthropist-he-qiaonv-commits-1-5-billion-to-conservation"},"containerId":"box-RXz25Mf"}</script>

        <p>&#x201C;[China is] pivoting to a new narrative in record speed,&#x201D; said Tom Kaplan, founder and chairman of <a href="https://www.panthera.org/" itemprop="StoryLink" itemscope="itemscope" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><meta content="WebLink" itemprop="type">Panthera</a>, the leading wild cat conservation organization and He&#x2019;s first international partner. &#x201C;Their [global] reputation has suffered by being viewed as the scourge of the elephant and tiger&#x2014;and they want to reverse this.&#x201D;</p><div class="regwall" data-position="4"></div><p>As part of their partnership, He&#x2019;s namesake <a href="http://en.cgpi.org.cn/content/details16_524.html" itemprop="StoryLink" itemscope="itemscope" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><meta content="WebLink" itemprop="type">Beijing Qiaonv Foundation</a> (BQF) is pledging $20 million toward&#xA0;Chinese snow leopard and other projects at Panthera&#x2014;significant for an organization whose annual operating budget hovers around $14 million. And doubly significant given that threatened cats in China had yet to be put under such a bright&#xA0;spotlight as, say, <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2016-08-16/see-and-help-save-namibia-s-desert-lions-before-it-s-too-late" itemprop="StoryLink" itemscope="itemscope" target="_blank"><meta content="StoryLink" itemprop="type">lions in Africa</a>.</p><p>With the emergence of Chinese leadership in this area, Kaplan says Qiaonv&#x2019;s pledge stands to change the face of cat conservation forever. &#x201C;One day this event may be seen as a watershed.&#x201D;</p><h2>Private Wealth, Public Commitments</h2><figure align="center" data-align="center" data-id="319128668" data-image-size="column" data-type="image" style="max-width: 2200px;" class="figure-expandable"><div class="image"><div id="lazy-img-319128668" class="lazy-img"><style>#lazy-img-319128668:before{padding-top:66.68181818181817%;}</style><img src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/iQi6Q6jJlkb8/v2/60x-1.jpg" data-native-src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/iQi6Q6jJlkb8/v2/-1x-1.jpg" class="lazy-img__image"></div></div><figcaption><div class="news-figure-caption-text caption">Madame He&#x2019;s work is possible thanks to public-private partnerships under China&#x2019;s progressive president, Xi Jinping.</div><div class="news-figure-credit credit">Photographer: Krisztian Bocsi/Bloomberg</div></figcaption></figure><p>In China, domestic private conservation work still requires the collaboration of the government, as private landownership&#x2014;and therefore, privately managed nature reserves&#x2014;are not allowed under Chinese law. But under Xi Jinping&#x2019;s leadership, these private-public partnerships are becoming possible.</p><p>Xi has emerged as an unlikely environmental leader after the U.S. dropped out of the Paris climate accord. Skeptics may think of this as rhetoric aimed at filling a political gap, but he has already made moves by <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2016/12/30/world/asia/china-ivory-ban-elephants.html?_r=0" itemprop="StoryLink" itemscope="itemscope" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><meta content="WebLink" itemprop="type">banning the illegal ivory trade</a> by the end of 2017, putting forth a long-term proposal t<meta content="WebLink" itemprop="type">o <a href="http://money.cnn.com/2017/09/11/news/china-gas-electric-car-ban/index.html" itemprop="StoryLink" itemscope="itemscope" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">eliminate&#xA0;gasoline-powered cars</a>, and creating the country&#x2019;s first tiger and Amur leopard reserve near the Russian border. It&#x2019;s one of 30 to 50 new conservation zones the government has promised by 2020.&#xA0;</p>
        <div id="in-article-owkbVJm" class="page-ad" data-position="in-article">
            <script class="params" type="application/json">{"contentId":"OXQICA6S972A01","position":"in-article","dimensions":{"mobile":[[5,19],[300,250],[1,1]],"tablet":[[5,19],[728,90],[1,1]]},"type":"In Article Flex Native Ad","positionIncrement":1,"targeting":{"position":"in-article","positionIncrement":1,"url":"/news/articles/2017-10-12/beijing-philanthropist-he-qiaonv-commits-1-5-billion-to-conservation"},"containerId":"in-article-owkbVJm"}</script>
            <div class="in-article-ad-dummy"></div>

        <p>All this stands in sharp contrast with other realities in China: Combating air pollution, the most visible sign of China&#x2019;s environmental issues, <a href="/news/terminal/OV5VBY3H0JK0" itemprop="StoryLink" itemscope="itemscope" target="_blank" class="terminal-news-story" rel="nofollow noopener"><meta content="StoryLink" itemprop="type">continues to be a work in progress</a>. And the country is still <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/view/articles/2017-04-04/how-china-can-become-a-true-climate-leader" itemprop="StoryLink" itemscope="itemscope" target="_blank"><meta content="StoryLink" itemprop="type">a major proponent of coal</a>, despite cuts to its <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2016-03-05/china-sets-energy-consumption-cap-that-researcher-deems-loose" itemprop="StoryLink" itemscope="itemscope" target="_blank"><meta content="StoryLink" itemprop="type">overall energy consumption</a>.</p><p>But change is underway. &#x201C;When the Chinese government decides to do something, they do it,&#x201D; Kathryn Sheridan, CEO of a Brussels-based sustainability communications consultancy, <a href="https://www.reuters.com/article/us-environment-china/in-china-64-percent-say-they-are-environmentalists-report-idUSKBN0DN01H20140507" itemprop="StoryLink" itemscope="itemscope" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">told Reuters</a>. &#x201C;It&#x2019;s not the talking shop that we see in Europe.&#x201D;</p><p>Kaplan likens this moment in Chinese history to 19th century America, when the U.S. was making the move from rural to industrial society. &#x201C;The water and air were being polluted in the rush for economic growth, and wildlife was obliterated&#x2014;we nearly destroyed our own national symbol, the bald eagle,&#x201D; he explained. &#x201C;No nation has a monopoly on virtue, but it is also true that we can learn from history. The Chinese are experts at precisely that.&#x201D;</p><p>Madame He agrees that the collaboration of China&#x2019;s ultra-rich with their government marks a turning point for the country. &#x201C;The public awareness of environmental protection is gradually increasing in China,&#x201D; she told Bloomberg.&#xA0;</p><h2>From Landscaper to Global Conservationist</h2><figure align="center" data-align="center" data-id="319128803" data-image-size="column" data-type="image" style="max-width: 2200px;" class="figure-expandable"><div class="image"><div id="lazy-img-319128803" class="lazy-img"><style>#lazy-img-319128803:before{padding-top:63.54545454545455%;}</style><img src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/i6G1BFrGE3QI/v2/60x-1.jpg" data-native-src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/i6G1BFrGE3QI/v2/-1x-1.jpg" class="lazy-img__image"></div></div><figcaption><div class="news-figure-caption-text caption">A Siberian tiger at the Hengdaohezi Breeding Center in&#xA0;Heilongjiang province, China.</div><div class="news-figure-credit credit">Photographer: China Photos/Getty Images AsiaPac</div></figcaption></figure><p>Madame He&#x2019;s affinity for the environment is what drove her into landscaping and resource management in the first place. But her vision for how she could contribute toward&#xA0;the greater good of the planet has evolved over time.</p><p>&#x201C;At the very beginning, the dream of our business was to build 100 of the most beautiful parks in 100 cities of China,&#x201D; said He of <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/research/stocks/private/snapshot.asp?privcapid=51181490" itemprop="StoryLink" itemscope="itemscope" target="_blank"><meta content="WebLink" itemprop="type">Beijing Orient Landscape Co.</a>, the company she built from scratch and continues to oversee as chairman. What she found along the way were polluted water systems and depleted urban ecology.</p><p>In 2012&#xA0;she founded Beijing Qiaonv Foundation with the goal of resolving some of the world&#x2019;s most pressing environmental issues. Among her priorities were establishing key conservation areas within her home country; identifying native species in the greatest need of protection; and lobbying the government, partnering with international organizations, and supporting domestic NGOs to create meaningful change that could impact global biodiversity and carbon dioxide levels.</p><p>As He put it, &#x201C;We believe that protecting China is to protect the whole Earth.&#x201D;</p><p>Private investments like hers matter. As we see <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2017-10-06/trump-quietly-stalls-safeguards-for-dozens-of-endangered-species" itemprop="StoryLink" itemscope="itemscope" target="_blank"><meta content="StoryLink" itemprop="type">happening now in the U.S.</a>, official Chinese priorities could easily shift, unraveling or putting a halt to progress that was quickly made. Partnerships with global players such as Kaplan and Bill Gates, who has worked with He through the <a href="http://en.cgpi.org.cn/index.html" itemprop="StoryLink" itemscope="itemscope" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><meta content="WebLink" itemprop="type">China Global Philanthropy Institute</a> (CGPI), mean that He&#x2019;s fundraising commitments are being given extra measures of accountability. Just as important, they&#x2019;re also being given a proven toolkit with which to succeed.</p><h2>A Training Kit Imported From the West</h2><figure align="center" data-align="center" data-id="319128872" data-image-size="column" data-type="image" style="max-width: 2200px;" class="figure-expandable"><div class="image"><div id="lazy-img-319128872" class="lazy-img"><style>#lazy-img-319128872:before{padding-top:66.63636363636364%;}</style><img src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/ifySofg.acg8/v1/60x-1.jpg" data-native-src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/ifySofg.acg8/v1/-1x-1.jpg" class="lazy-img__image"></div></div><figcaption><div class="news-figure-caption-text caption">A snow leopard, whose habitat is Central Asia.&#xA0;</div><div class="news-figure-credit credit">Photographer: Thomas Kitchin Victoria Hurst/First Light</div></figcaption></figure><p>With 79 projects already underway in 26 provinces&#x2014;including everything from Asian elephant conservation to wetland protection&#x2014;BQF didn&#x2019;t need international validation or support to start making a difference. But at last year&#x2019;s East-West Sustainability Summit in Honolulu, which was convened in partnership with&#xA0;CGPI, He shared a table with some of the world&#x2019;s biggest players in conservation, including Nicole Mollo, the executive director of environmental philanthropy at the Recanati-Kaplan Foundation, the Panthera&#xA0;founder&#x2019;s private organization.</p><p>&#x201C;Things [in China] are changing under the global radar,&#x201D; said Mollo, who went on to broker the partnership between Panthera and BQF, helping to establish both the financial scope and environmental goals that the partnership would support. &#x201C;They have the will and frankly they have the resources&#x2014;what they are missing is a middle tier of expertise. They don&#x2019;t know what it means to manage a protected area, to train a ranger, or to work with communities and livestock.&#x201D;</p><p>That&#x2019;s why the partnership with Panthera marks a meaningful shift in He&#x2019;s work: With the organization&#x2019;s help, BQF will create and staff two protected snow leopard reserves that will serve as pilot areas and can be scaled over time, while simultaneously underwriting a wildlife management training program for Chinese conservationists. Then she&#x2019;ll turn her attention to building hundreds of urban classrooms where &#x201C;hundreds of millions of people can visit and learn&#x201D; about conservation.</p><h2>Big Goals, Big Impact</h2><figure align="center" data-align="center" data-id="319128901" data-image-size="column" data-type="image" style="max-width: 2200px;" class="figure-expandable"><div class="image"><div id="lazy-img-319128901" class="lazy-img"><style>#lazy-img-319128901:before{padding-top:66.68181818181817%;}</style><img src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/iOB3esT6Md.o/v1/60x-1.jpg" data-native-src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/iOB3esT6Md.o/v1/-1x-1.jpg" class="lazy-img__image"></div></div><figcaption><div class="news-figure-caption-text caption">Snow leopard in winter.</div><div class="news-figure-credit credit">Photographer: through-my-lens/iStockphoto</div></figcaption></figure><p>Even if He only accomplishes a quarter of what she sets out to do with her $1.5 billion pledge, she stands to make a massive impact.</p><p>In some ways, she already has. While the $20 million contribution to Panthera&#xA0;matches the commitments made by several global figureheads&#x2014;Mohammed bin Zayed, the crown prince of Abu Dhabi&#x2014;a 10-figure pledge is&#xA0;&#x201C;unheard of.&#x201D; That&#x2019;s according to&#xA0;Mollo, who has facilitated some of the largest recorded contributions to conservation organizations over her years at the African Parks Foundation and the Wildlife Conservation Society.</p><aside class="inline-newsletter" data-state="ready"></aside><p>She said the largest donation she&#x2019;s seen on record was <a href="http://www.wyssfoundation.org/news/the-wyss-foundation-commits-65m-to-african-parks-to-secure-protected-areas-in-africa" itemprop="StoryLink" itemscope="itemscope" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><meta content="WebLink" itemprop="type">a $65 million commitment over 10 years</a>; pledges in the hundreds of millions, like Gates&#x2019;s&#xA0;<a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2017-08-14/gates-makes-largest-donation-since-2000-with-4-6-billion-pledge" itemprop="StoryLink" itemscope="itemscope" target="_blank"><meta content="StoryLink" itemprop="type">recent $5 billion pledge</a> to his health care- and education-focused foundation, are generally made to universities, hospitals, or cultural institutions with naming opportunities attached to them. In the conservation world? It&#x2019;s not something she&#x2019;d ever seen before. To wit, a spokesperson for the National Audubon Society confirmed that the organization&#x2019;s recent &#x201C;large donations&#x201D; have rarely reached the seven-figure level&#x2014;and its largest contributions have come from groups like the MacArthur Foundation rather than individuals.</p><p>Added Mollo, &#x201C;I would be the first one to bash China for what they&#x2019;ve done wrong, but that strategy will not get us anywhere. And when they put their money where their mouth is, it is our job to support them.&#x201D;</p><ol class="noscript-footnotes"></ol>
